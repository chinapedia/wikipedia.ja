**チャンバル川**（, ）は[インド](../Page/インド.md "wikilink")の[川](../Page/川.md "wikilink")で、国の中央部を流れる[ヤムナー川](../Page/ヤムナー川.md "wikilink")の支流である。[マディヤ・プラデーシュ州](../Page/マディヤ・プラデーシュ州.md "wikilink")と[ラージャスターン州](../Page/ラージャスターン州.md "wikilink")を流れ、全長は1024 キロメートル、流域面積は14万3,219平方キロメートルである。チャンバル川は、マディヤ・プラデーシュ州の西部にあるとの境に起源がある。

## Origin, drainage and mouth

[Chambal_River_near_Kota,_Rajasthan.jpg](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Chambal_River_near_Kota,_Rajasthan.jpg "fig:Chambal_River_near_Kota,_Rajasthan.jpg")\]\] The long Chambal River originates from the Singar Chouri peak on the northern slopes of the [Vindhyan escarpment](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Vindhyas "wikilink") near [:en:Mandav](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Mandav "wikilink"), South-West of [:en:Mhow](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Mhow "wikilink") in [:en:Indore](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Indore "wikilink") District, [:en:Madhya Pradesh](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Madhya_Pradesh "wikilink") state, at an elevation of about . The river flows first in a northerly direction through Madhya Pradesh (M.P.) for about  and then in a generally north-easterly direction for  through [:en:Rajasthan](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Rajasthan "wikilink"). The Chambal flows for another  between M.P. and Rajasthan and a further  between M.P. and [Uttar Pradesh](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh "wikilink")(U.P.). It enters U.P. and flows for about  before joining the [:en:Yamuna River](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Yamuna_River "wikilink") in [:en:Jalaun](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Jalaun "wikilink") District at an elevation of , to form a part of the greater Gangetic drainage system.\[1\]

From its source down to its junction with the Yamuna, the Chambal has a fall of about . Of this, around  is within the first  reach from its source. It falls for another  in the next , where it enters the gorge past the Chaurasigarh Fort. During the next  of its run from the Chaurasigarh Fort to [Kota](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Kota_District "wikilink") city, the bed falls by another . For the rest of its  run, the river passes through the flat terrain of the [:en:Malwa Plateau](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Malwa_Plateau "wikilink") and later the [:en:Gangetic Plain](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Gangetic_Plain "wikilink") with an average gradient of 0.21 m/km.\[2\]

The Chambal is a rainfed catchment with a total drained area up to its confluence with the Yamuna of . The drainage area resembles a rectangle up to the junction of the Parvathi and Banas Rivers with the Chambal flowing along its major axis. The Chambal Basin lies between latitudes 22° 27' N and 27° 20' N and longitudes 73° 20' E and 79° 15' E. On its south, east and west, the basin is bounded by the Vindhyan mountain ranges and on the north-west by the [Aravallis](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Aravalli_Range "wikilink"). Below the confluence of the Parvathi and Banas, the catchment becomes narrower and elongated. In this reach, it is bounded by the Aravalli mountain ranges on the North and the Vindhyan hill range on the south.\[3\]

The Vindhyan scarps, in the northwest, flank the left bank of the Chambal, and subsequently, is mainly drained by it. The Chambal rising within about 16 km of the Narmada river, appears as a consequent on the Mesozoic surface, superimposed on the scarps, and cuts straight through them, with subsequent tributaries on the softer shales. The River Chambal and its tributaries [:en:Kali Sindh](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Kali_Sindh "wikilink") and [:en:Parbati](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Parbati "wikilink") have formed a triangular alluvial basin, about  above the narrow trough of the lower Chambal in [Kota](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Kota_District "wikilink"). It is a typical anterior-drainage pattern river, being much older than the rivers Yamuna and Ganges, into which it eventually flows.\[4\]

The tributaries of the Chambal include [:en:Shipra](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Shipra "wikilink"), Choti Kalisindh, Sivanna, Retam, Ansar, Kalisindh, Banas, Parbati, Seep, Kuwari, Kuno, Alnia, Mej, Chakan, Parwati, Chamla, Gambhir, Lakhunder, Khan, Bangeri, Kedel and Teelar.\[5\]\[6\]

According to Crawford (1969), the Chambal river valley is part of the Vindhyan system which consists of massive sandstone, slate and limestone, of perhaps pre-Cambrian age, resting on the surface of older rocks.\[7\] Hillocks and plateaus represent the major landforms of the Chambal valley. The Chambal basin is characterised by an undulating floodplain, gullies and ravines.\[8\] The Hadauti plateau in Rajasthan occurs in the upper catchment of the Chambal River to the southeast of the Mewar Plains. It occurs with the Malwa plateau in the east. Physiographically, it can be divided into Vindhyan scarp land and Deccan Lava (Malwa) plateau.\[9\] According to Heron (1953), the eastern pediplain, occurring between the Vindhyan plateau and the [Aravalli hill range](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Aravalli_Hill_Range "wikilink"), contains a thin veneer of Quaternary sediments, reworked soil and river channel fills. At least two erosional surfaces can be recognised within the pediplain are the Tertiary age. The Vindhyan upland, the adjoining Chambal valley and the Indo-Gangetic alluvial tract (older alluvium) are of Pleistocene to Sub-recent age. Badland topography is a characteristic feature of the Chambal valley, whereas kankar has extensively developed in the older alluvium.\[10\]

## National Chambal Sanctuary

[Indian_Skimmer_(8582799716).jpg](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Indian_Skimmer_\(8582799716\).jpg "fig:Indian_Skimmer_(8582799716).jpg") The National Chambal Sanctuary lies between 24°55' to 26°50' N and 75°34' to 79°18'E in [:en:Dholpur](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Dholpur "wikilink"). It consists of the large arc described by the Chambal between Jawahar Sagar Dam in Rajasthan and the Chambal-Yamuna confluence in Uttar Pradesh. Over this arc, two stretches of the Chambal are protected as the National Chambal Sanctuary status - the upper sector, extending from Jawahar Sagar Dam to Kota Barrage, and the lower sector, extending from Keshoraipatan in Rajasthan to the Chambal-Yamuna confluence in Uttar Pradesh.\[11\]

The sanctuary was gazetted 'in order to facilitate the restoration to "ecological health" of a major north Indian river system and provide full protection for the gravely endangered [:en:gharial](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:gharial "wikilink") (*Gavialis gangeticus*).\[12\]

Administrative approval of the Government of India for the establishment of the National Chambal Sanctuary was conveyed in Order No. 17-74/77-FRY (WL) dated 30 September 1978. The Sanctuary has sanctuary status declared under Section 18(1) of the Wildlife Protection Act, 1972. Since such a declaration is carried out by individual states for territory falling within their jurisdiction, there are three separate notifications covering the National Chambal Sanctuary - the Madhya Pradesh portion was gazetted in the Government of Madhya Pradesh Notice No. F.15/5/77-10(2) dated 20 December 1978, the Uttar Pradesh portion was gazetted in the Government of Uttar Pradesh Notice No. 7835/XIV-3-103-78 dated 29 January 1979 and the Rajasthan portion was gazetted in the Government of Rajasthan Notice No.F.11(12)Rev.8/78 dated 7 December 1979.\[13\]

## Mythology

The ancient name of the Chambal was *Charmanvati*, meaning the river on whose banks leather is dried. In due course of time, this river became famous as the river of ‘charman’ (skin) and was named as *Charmanvati*.\[14\]

The epic [:en:Sanskrit](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Sanskrit "wikilink") narrative the [:en:Mahabharata](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Mahabharata "wikilink"), refers to the Chambal river as the *[Charmanyavati](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Charmanwati "wikilink")* : originating from the blood of thousands of animals sacrificed by the Aryan King [:en:Rantideva](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Rantideva "wikilink").\[15\]

  -
    "So large was the number of animals sacrificed in the [:en:Agnihotra](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Agnihotra "wikilink") of that king that the secretions flowing from his kitchen from the heaps of skins deposited there caused a veritable river which from this circumstance, came to be called the *Charmanwati*.<ref name="AV">

</ref>"

Charmanwati was the southern boundary of [Panchala Kingdom](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Panchala_Kingdom_\(Mahabharata\) "wikilink"). King [:en:Drupada](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Drupada "wikilink") ruled the southern Panchalas up to the bank of the Charmanwati river (1:140).

According to folklore the Chambal area was part of [:en:Shakuni](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Shakuni "wikilink")’s kingdom and the dice-game played thereabouts. After the [attempted disrobing](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Draupadi#The_attempted_stripping "wikilink") of [:en:Draupadi](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/:en:Draupadi "wikilink") (the daughter of Drupada) she cursed any one who would drink the water of the Charmanwati river.\[16\] Thus it is believed that due to the curse by Draupadi, have helped the Chambal to survive unpolluted by man, and its many animal inhabitants to thrive relatively untouched. The Chambal remains one of India’s most pristine rivers.\[17\]\[18\]
